## CHAPTER 6: The Compass — Learning to Read the Currents

---

There is something I have not yet told you.

Everything I have described so far — the Loop, the Tiger, the Fog, the Clearing — I have described as if they are places you visit one at a time. As if you wake up in one quadrant, stay there for a while, and then move to another. As if the map is a chessboard and you are a single piece occupying one square.

This is not how it works.

In the course of a single hour, you may pass through all four quadrants. In the course of a single conversation, you may shift from the Clearing to the Loop and back again three times. In the course of a single breath — if you are paying very close attention — you may feel the currents pulling you in two directions at once, the energy rising while the attention turns inward, or the energy falling while the senses open, and for a fleeting, disorienting moment, you are between states, belonging to no quadrant, suspended in the space where one configuration dissolves and another has not yet formed.

This is the reality of inner life. It is not a landscape of fixed territories. It is a weather system — constantly shifting, endlessly dynamic, shaped by forces both visible and invisible. And you are not a tourist visiting its landmarks. You are a sailor on its sea, and the sea never stops moving.

The previous chapters gave you the map. This chapter teaches you to sail.

---

### Why You Cannot Feel What You Feel

Before I teach you to read the currents, I must address the obstacle that prevents most people from reading them at all.

The obstacle is not complexity. The currents are, at their most basic, only two variables — energy and direction. A child could understand the concept. The obstacle is something far more fundamental.

You have been trained, from birth, to ignore the currents.

Consider how you were raised. When you cried as a child — when the first current surged and the body expressed its distress through the only channel available — what happened? In many cases, you were told to stop. "Don't cry." "Be brave." "There's nothing to cry about." The message, delivered with varying degrees of kindness, was clear: *what you are feeling is not appropriate. Suppress it.*

When you were afraid — when the energy spiked and the direction turned inward — you may have been told that your fear was irrational. "There's nothing under the bed." "Don't be silly." The message: *your internal reading of the situation is wrong. Override it.*

When you were exhausted — when the first current dropped and the body asked for rest — you may have been told to push through. "You can sleep when you're dead." "Don't be lazy." The message: *the signal your body is sending is an inconvenience. Ignore it.*

Over years, over thousands of repetitions, you learned the lesson. You learned to *not feel what you feel.* Not to suppress feelings after noticing them — that would at least preserve the noticing. You learned something more radical: to not notice them in the first place. The signals are still there. The body is still broadcasting. But you have turned off the receiver.

This is what makes the map, for many people, initially useless. I can tell you that there are two currents — energy and direction — and that your task is to notice where they are at any given moment. But if you have spent decades training yourself to *not notice,* the instruction "just notice" is like telling a person who has been wearing earplugs for forty years to "just listen."

The earplugs must come out first. And removing them is not a single act. It is a process — gentle, gradual, and sometimes deeply uncomfortable, because the signals you have been blocking are not all pleasant. Some of them are very loud. Some of them carry old pain. Some of them will, when first allowed through, feel like an assault.

This is why so many people resist the inner work. Not because they are lazy or unspiritual or afraid of the truth. Because at some deep, pre-verbal level, the body remembers that the last time it fully felt what it felt, something bad happened. Someone dismissed it. Someone punished it. Someone left.

And so the body made a deal: *I will stop telling you what I feel, and in return, you will stop being hurt for feeling it.*

This deal, which was necessary for survival in childhood, becomes a prison in adulthood. Because now you are navigating a sea without instruments. You have no compass. The currents toss you from quadrant to quadrant, and you do not know where you are until you are already deep inside a Loop or buried in a Fog or crashing out of a Tiger state, and by then the momentum is enormous and the correction is difficult.

The compass I am about to teach you is the reversal of this deal. It is the slow, patient, courageous reopening of the channel between the body and the awareness. It is the willingness to feel what you feel — not to *act* on it, not to *believe* every story it generates, but simply to *register* it. To know, at any given moment, the answer to two questions:

*How much energy is in my system right now?*

*Where is my attention pointing?*

That is the entire compass. Two readings. Two numbers on two scales. And they change everything.

### The First Reading: Energy

Let me teach you how to take the first reading.

Stop reading for a moment. Close your eyes if you like, or soften your gaze. Do not try to relax. Do not try to meditate. Do not try to do anything at all.

Simply ask: **How activated is my body right now?**

Do not answer with a word. Words come from the Storying System and will contaminate the reading. Answer with a *felt sense* — a direct, nonverbal impression of the body's current activation level.

Is there tension? Where? In the jaw, the shoulders, the belly, the hands? Tension is a sign of elevated energy — the muscles are braced for action, even if no action is required.

Is there heaviness? Where? In the limbs, the eyelids, the chest? Heaviness is a sign of depleted energy — the system is running on reserve, conserving what little it has.

Is the heartbeat noticeable? If you can feel your heart without trying — if it is pounding or racing or thumping against the chest wall — the energy is high. If you cannot feel it at all, the energy is either very low or moderate.

Is the breath fast or slow? Shallow or deep? Held or flowing? The breath is the most accessible window into the first current because it is the only autonomic function that is *also* under voluntary control. It sits at the intersection of the unconscious and the conscious, the body and the mind. It is both a reading and a lever — it tells you where the energy is, and it gives you the ability to shift it.

You do not need a number. You do not need precision. You need only a rough sense — a *direction on the dial.* Is the energy high? Low? Somewhere in the middle? This rough reading, taken honestly, is more valuable than a thousand hours of abstract self-analysis.

Because the reading tells you *what is possible.*

When the energy is very high, certain things are possible and others are not. Intense focus is possible. Decisive action is possible. But calm reflection is not possible. Nuanced social connection is not possible. Creative exploration is not possible. The bandwidth has been allocated to survival, and survival does not share.

When the energy is very low, a different set of possibilities opens and closes. Rest is possible. Gentle sensory awareness is possible. But vigorous engagement is not possible. Complex problem-solving is not possible. Sustained effort is not possible. The system is in conservation mode, and conservation does not invest.

Most of the frustration in your life — and most of the frustration in the lives of the people I have worked with for thirty years — comes from *trying to do something that your current energy state does not support.*

You try to meditate when your energy is screaming. You try to perform when your energy is gone. You try to have a calm, connected conversation with your partner when your body is braced for battle. You try to be creative when your system is running on fumes.

And when these attempts fail — as they must, because you are asking the organism to do something it is physiologically incapable of doing in its current state — you conclude that *you* have failed. That you are not disciplined enough, not spiritual enough, not strong enough.

You are not failing. You are *misreading the instrument.* You are trying to sail north in a south wind and blaming yourself for not making progress.

The first act of self-compassion — the one that makes all other acts of self-compassion possible — is to take the reading and *believe it.* To accept that the body's current state is not a moral judgment but a *weather report.* And to adjust your plans accordingly.

When the energy is high: do not try to be still. *Use the energy.* Move. Act. Engage with something demanding enough to absorb the activation. Or, if the energy is uncomfortably high, bring it down first — one breath, the mechanical brake — before attempting anything that requires calm.

When the energy is low: do not try to perform. *Honor the depletion.* Rest. Receive. Let the system restore itself. Or, if you must act, begin with the gentlest possible movement — a walk, a stretch, a single small task that asks nothing of the depleted system except to begin.

When the energy is moderate: this is the window. This is when the full range of human experience is available — thought, feeling, action, connection, creativity, rest. Do not waste this window on activities that do not require it. Save the difficult conversation for moderate energy. Save the creative work for moderate energy. Save the deep reflection for moderate energy. These are the tasks that require *all* of you, and "all of you" is only available when the first current is neither surging nor depleted but flowing at its natural, sustainable pace.

### The Second Reading: Direction

Now the second question. This one is subtler, because the direction of attention is less obviously physical than the level of energy. But it is equally readable, once you know what to look for.

**Where is my attention pointing right now?**

Again, do not answer with a word. Answer with a *noticing.*

Are you in a story? Is there a narrative running — about the past, the future, about yourself, about someone else? Are you rehearsing, replaying, planning, worrying, fantasizing? Is the "movie" playing?

Or are you in the room? Can you feel the chair beneath you? Can you hear the ambient sound? Is the world *present* — vivid, textured, here — or is it a vague backdrop behind the more compelling drama of the inner theater?

The answer will almost always be one or the other. The brain's competitive inhibition — the seesaw between the Storying System and the Sensing System — ensures that you are rarely in both simultaneously. You are either *in the thought* or *in the room.* In the narrative or in the world. On the left side of the map or the right.

And here is a subtlety that my earlier books did not address: **there is no shame in being on the left side.**

I inadvertently created, in some of my readers, a fear of the inner world. A belief that any thought is a failure, any mental activity is ego, any engagement with the Storying System is a fall from grace. People who had read *The Power of Now* would catch themselves thinking and feel a stab of guilt — "I was lost in thought again, I'm not present, I'm failing at awakening" — which is, of course, just another thought, another story, another Loop.

The Storying System is not your enemy. It is a *tool* — the most powerful tool evolution has ever produced. It allows you to remember the past, which means you can learn from experience. It allows you to simulate the future, which means you can plan and prepare. It allows you to construct abstract models of reality, which means you can solve problems that have never been encountered before. It allows you to imagine the inner lives of other people, which means you can empathize, cooperate, and love.

The problem was never that you have a Storying System. The problem was that the Storying System was running *unsupervised.* It was operating without oversight, without the ability to be interrupted, without the awareness that it is a *tool being used* rather than a *self doing the using.*

The compass does not ask you to destroy the Storying System. It asks you to *know when it is active.* That is all. Just the knowing. Just the awareness: "Ah — I am in a story right now."

This knowing — this momentary meta-awareness, this flicker of recognition — is the single most transformative act a human being can perform. Not because it does anything dramatic. But because it *separates the observer from the observed.* For one instant, "I" am not the story. "I" am the one noticing the story. And in that instant, the spell breaks. Not permanently. Not completely. But enough to create a gap — a tiny space of freedom between the stimulus and the response — in which a different choice becomes possible.

You can choose to stay in the story, if the story is useful. Planning dinner is a story. Solving a math problem is a story. Writing a letter to a friend is a story. These are good uses of the Storying System, and there is no reason to interrupt them.

Or you can choose to return to the room. To feel the chair, hear the sound, see the light. To slide your attention back to the right side of the map, where the world is waiting, unchanged, undamaged, perfectly present.

The choice itself is the freedom. Not the outcome of the choice. The *fact that you have it.*

### The Compass in Practice

Let me show you what this looks like in real time.

You are sitting at your desk. You have been working for an hour. You take the reading.

**Energy:** Moderate-high. There is some tension in the shoulders. The breath is a bit shallow. The heartbeat is noticeable but not racing. The system is activated but not overwhelmed.

**Direction:** Inward. You have been deep in a planning document — a useful story, a productive engagement of the Storying System. But you notice that the planning has drifted. You are no longer planning tomorrow's meeting. You are *worrying* about tomorrow's meeting. The content has shifted from "What needs to happen" to "What could go wrong." The emotional tone has shifted from purposeful to anxious.

This is the moment. The compass has told you two things: your energy is rising toward the upper boundary of productive activation, and your attention has slipped from useful storytelling to unproductive rumination. You are drifting from the center of the map toward the upper-left quadrant. Toward the Loop.

You are not in the Loop yet. The current has only just begun to turn. And because you noticed it *now* — before the momentum became overwhelming — the correction is small. Almost effortless.

One breath. The double inhale, the long exhale. The energy drops one notch. The shoulders release a fraction of their tension.

Then: a glance out the window. Not a heroic act of mindfulness. A glance. The sky is there. A tree is there. A car passes on the street below. The senses receive these impressions — light, color, movement — and for a few seconds, the Sensing System activates, and the worry-narrative pauses.

You return to your work. The planning resumes — but from a slightly calmer, slightly clearer baseline. The drift toward the Loop has been corrected. Not by force. Not by spiritual willpower. By a compass reading and a small adjustment.

This took less than thirty seconds.

Now multiply this by a hundred. A hundred small readings, a hundred small corrections, spread across the hours of a single day. None of them dramatic. None of them visible to anyone else. None of them requiring you to stop your life and sit on a cushion for forty minutes.

This is the practice. This is the spiritual life as it is actually lived — not in retreats and breakthroughs and peak experiences, but in the *accumulated weight of a thousand tiny navigations,* each one almost too small to notice, each one shifting the trajectory by a single degree, and all of them together bending the arc of a human life from unconscious reaction toward conscious response.

### The Rhythm of the Day

Once you begin taking regular readings, you will discover something that no one told you but that your body has always known: **the currents have a rhythm.**

Energy is not constant. It rises and falls in waves throughout the day — not randomly, but in a pattern shaped by biology, by light, by food, by activity, by the deep circadian architecture that governs all living things.

For most people, energy rises in the morning, peaks in the late morning or early afternoon, dips in the mid-afternoon, rises again briefly in the early evening, and then descends toward sleep. This is not a flaw. It is a design. The organism is built to oscillate, to pulse, to alternate between activation and rest. The wave is the signature of a healthy system.

But the modern world does not respect the wave. The modern world demands a flat line — constant productivity, constant availability, constant output from 7 a.m. to 11 p.m. And when the body's natural rhythm dips, as it must, the modern person does not dip with it. They override it. Coffee at 3 p.m. Email at 9 p.m. Screens at midnight. The wave is flattened, and in its place, a relentless, artificial plateau is maintained — not through genuine energy but through stimulants, willpower, and the chronic suppression of the body's signal to rest.

This is the recipe for the Fog. The body, denied its natural oscillation, eventually stops oscillating. The wave flattens not into the productive plateau the person intended but into a low, gray, undifferentiated monotone. The system, overridden too many times, stops sending signals. It stops asking for rest because rest was never granted. It stops surging in the morning because the surge was never discharged through genuine engagement. It becomes — to use the technical term — *dysregulated.* The rhythm is lost.

The compass restores the rhythm. Not by imposing a schedule — the body does not respond well to imposed schedules, which are just another form of override — but by *listening.* By taking the reading and honoring it.

When the energy is high: use it. This is the time for the Tiger — for the demanding work, the difficult conversation, the creative project that requires your full engagement. Do not waste high energy on email. Do not waste it on tasks that a lower-energy version of you could handle just as well. High energy is a finite resource. Spend it wisely.

When the energy dips: let it dip. This is the time for rest — or for the gentle, receptive activities that thrive on low activation. Read. Listen to music. Walk slowly. Sit with a friend. Or simply do nothing for fifteen minutes and let the system restore itself. The dip is not an interruption of productivity. It is the *foundation* of productivity. Without it, the next wave cannot rise.

When the energy is moderate: this is the Clearing. This is the time for the activities that require the full range of your humanity — connection, reflection, creativity, compassion. These activities do not thrive on adrenaline. They do not thrive on depletion. They thrive on the balanced, settled, open state that moderate energy provides.

Learn the rhythm. Ride the wave. Stop fighting the ocean.

### When the Compass Breaks

I must address one more thing, because honesty requires it.

There are times when the compass does not work.

There are times when you take the reading and get nothing. No sense of energy level. No awareness of attentional direction. Just a blank — a fog within the Fog, a numbness so complete that even the instrument of self-awareness has been swallowed by it.

This happens. It happens to meditators of forty years' experience. It happens to psychologists and neuroscientists and people who have read every book on the shelf. It happens because the system that generates self-awareness is itself *part of the system that can be disrupted.* When the disruption is severe enough — when the stress is acute enough, the trauma deep enough, the depletion profound enough — the observer goes offline along with everything else.

When this happens, you cannot navigate. You cannot take readings. You cannot make corrections. You are, for the duration, at the mercy of the currents.

And here — in this place of total helplessness — the most important teaching of this entire book applies.

**You do not need the compass to be safe.**

The compass is a tool. A magnificent tool. A tool that, when functioning, gives you a degree of agency over your inner life that most human beings throughout history have never had. But it is a tool. And tools can break. And when they break, you are not *nothing.* You are not lost. You are not abandoned by the universe.

You are a human being in a body on a planet, breathing air, subject to gravity, held by the same forces that hold the stars in their courses. And these forces do not require your awareness to operate. Your heart beats without your permission. Your lungs fill without your instruction. Your wounds heal while you sleep. The body's intelligence — the vast, ancient, wordless intelligence that has been sustaining life on this planet for four billion years — does not need your compass to do its work.

When the compass breaks, let it be broken. Do not add the suffering of "I should be more aware" to the suffering you are already experiencing. Do not turn the loss of the instrument into another story about your inadequacy. The compass will come back online when the system has stabilized. It always does. Not because you willed it back, but because awareness is the *nature* of consciousness, and nature, given half a chance, restores itself.

In the meantime, the most powerful thing you can do is the most primitive thing: attend to the body's basic needs. Drink water. Eat something simple. Lie down if you can. Go outside if you can. Touch a living thing — a tree, a dog, a human hand. These are not sophisticated interventions. They are the interventions that work when sophistication has failed. They are the ground floor of the building — the foundation that remains when every upper story has collapsed.

And from the ground floor, slowly, in its own time, the system rebuilds. The readings return. The compass flickers back to life. The currents become legible again. And you resume your navigation — humbler, perhaps, than before. More grateful for the instrument. More gentle with yourself when you lose it.

### The Compass and Compassion

I want to tell you the deepest thing the compass teaches. It is not about you.

When you learn to read your own currents — when you develop the habit of noticing your energy level and attentional direction throughout the day — something unexpected happens. You begin to read *other people's* currents.

You see your colleague snapping at everyone in the meeting, and instead of thinking, "What a difficult person," you read the compass: *high energy, internally directed. They are in the Loop. They are not seeing the room. They are seeing a threat that exists only in their narrative.*

You see your teenager lying on the couch, unresponsive, unreachable, refusing to engage, and instead of thinking, "What is wrong with this child?" you read the compass: *low energy, internally directed. They are in the Fog. They are not lazy. They are depleted. Or shut down. Or both.*

You see your partner pacing the kitchen, talking rapidly about a project, eyes bright, gestures animated, and instead of being swept into their energy or irritated by it, you read the compass: *high energy, externally directed. They are in the Tiger. This is not the moment for a conversation about feelings. This is the moment to let them run.*

You see a stranger on the bus, staring out the window with soft eyes, hands relaxed, breathing slowly, and you recognize the compass reading without needing to name it: *they are in the Clearing. They are home.*

This is not judgment. It is *recognition.* And recognition is the beginning of compassion.

Because when you understand that a person's behavior is not their *character* but their *current state* — not who they are but *where they are on the map* — everything changes. You stop taking their Loop personally. You stop resenting their Fog. You stop being threatened by their Tiger. You see them as navigators, just like you, caught in currents they may not even be aware of, doing the best they can with the instruments they have.

And sometimes — not always, not with everyone, but sometimes — you can help. Not by fixing them. Not by advising them. Not by telling them about the map. But by doing the simplest, most powerful thing one nervous system can do for another.

You can settle your own.

You can take your own reading, find your own center, and be — in their presence — a living demonstration that the Clearing exists. That safety is possible. That the currents can be navigated. That the storm, however fierce, is not the whole of the ocean.

You cannot give someone else your compass. But you can be a lighthouse.

And in a world full of storms, a lighthouse is no small thing.

### The Daily Practice

I want to leave you with a practice for this chapter. It is the simplest practice in this book and, if done consistently, the most transformative.

Three times a day — morning, midday, and evening — stop what you are doing for thirty seconds and take a reading.

**How much energy is in my system right now?** Feel it. Do not name it. Feel it.

**Where is my attention pointing?** Notice it. Do not judge it. Notice it.

That is all.

Do not try to change what you find. Do not try to steer anywhere. Just read the compass. Just know where you are.

Over days, you will begin to notice patterns. You will see when the energy tends to rise and when it tends to fall. You will see which situations pull your attention inward and which open it outward. You will see the early warning signs of the Loop — the subtle tightening, the first whisper of the worry-narrative — before it becomes a storm. You will see the early signs of the Fog — the dimming, the withdrawal, the glass descending — before it becomes a prison.

And in the seeing — in the simple, nonjudgmental, compassionate seeing — you will discover something that no amount of effort could produce.

The currents will begin to settle on their own.

Not because you forced them. Not because you mastered them. Because *awareness itself is regulatory.* The act of observing the system changes the system. Not dramatically. Not immediately. But persistently, cumulatively, in the way that water shapes stone — not through force but through presence.

You have always been on this sea. The currents have always been moving. What changes, when you pick up the compass, is not the sea.

It is your relationship to it.
